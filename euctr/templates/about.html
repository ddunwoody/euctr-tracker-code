About the EU Trials Tracker

Non-reporting of clinical trial results is an ongoing global public health problem.

By EU law, from December 2016, all trials on the European Union Clinical Trials Register (EUCTR) must post results within 12 months of completion of the trial. This is the first legislation enacted anywhere in the world which requires this.

EU Trials Tracker shows which organisations are compliant and which aren't.

What's a clinical trial?

To find out if a new drug or medical treatment actually helps patients, it is vital to carefully test it in real use. A clinical trial can involve hundreds of people in half a dozen countries, and run for several years.

Why's it so important that outcomes are reported?

At the outset, those conducting clinical trials are supposed to publicly declare what measurements they will take to assess the relative benefits of the treatments being compared. This is long-standing best practice, because an outcome such as ‘cardiovascular health’ could be measured in many ways. So researchers are expected to list the specific blood tests and symptom-rating scales that they will use, for example, alongside the dates on which measurements will be taken, and any cut-off values they will apply to turn continuous data into categorical variables.

This is all done to prevent researchers from ‘data-dredging’ their results. If researchers switch from these pre-specified outcomes, without explaining that they have done so, then they break the assumptions of their statistical tests. That carries a significant risk of exaggerating findings, or simply getting them wrong, and this in turn helps to explain why so many trial results eventually turn out to be incorrect.

Where can I read the journal paper about this?

Full details of our research is published in the academic paper "Failure to Comply with Results-Reporting Requirements on the EU Clinical Trials Register: a Cohort Study" by Ben Goldacre, Nicholas J DeVito, Carl Heneghan, Seb Bacon, Jessica Fleminger, Helen Curtis and Open Knowledge International.

Who made the EU Trials Tracker?

The Evidence-Based Medicine Data Lab at Oxford University. Francis Irving made the site with Ben Goldacre, Nick DeVito, Seb Bacon, Helen Curtis and Lisa French.

What does "due" mean?

When more than a year has passed since the completion of a trial, the results should be published, and we call it "due".

Specifically, we take the last global end of trial data listed for any of the trial's protocols. Then add a year (the legal limit) and 3 weeks (time for results to get published on the register after filing). If that is earlier than the date the data was updated, then the trial is due.

What does "inconsistent data" mean for a trial?

Some trials have problematic data on the registry. There are three reasons for this:

  1. All the sites are listed as completed, so there should be a completion date; and yet there is no completion date for the trial.
  2. There is a date for the “global end of the trial”; and yet some of the sites are still listed as ongoing.
  3. For some, the trial status is left entirely blank.

These errors may be due to trialists’ errors, or failures at the registry. However, we think the EU registry managers could and should automatically identify trials with inconsistent data, as we have done; and then require trialists to correct their records. More details are in our paper.

What happens when sponsors merge or split?

Over time organisations can merge, or split.

If we think an organisation is now effectively part of another, we say so at the bottom of its page.

Conversely, if an organisation now effectively includes other organisations, and so is responsible for their trials, we list those at the bottom of its page. The trials of the smaller organisations are not included in the counts of the larger one.

We've made every effort to get these relationships right. However, if they are wrong, please tell us.
